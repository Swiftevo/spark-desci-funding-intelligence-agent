## Executive Summary

ANTS Journal proposes an art journal positioned as a 'prototype for the future of artistic publishing' that bridges art, nature, technology, and science from local and indigenous perspectives. It is part of the Meta Gallery initiative based in Rio de Janeiro. The project emphasizes knowledge equity, decolonial narratives, and community-driven storytelling. While the curatorial vision and network are referenced, the proposal lacks concrete deliverables, measurable milestones, and evidence of published output. The project sits at the intersection of cultural publishing and DeSci but provides insufficient detail on execution, sustainability, and impact measurement.

## Risk Flags

- [high] Vague deliverables and milestones: The proposal provides no concrete deliverables, publication schedule, or measurable outcomes. 'Network' as progress is unverifiable. Reviewers cannot assess what funding would actually produce.
- [high] Unsubstantiated impact claims: Claims of 'challenging dominant global narratives' and 'helping communities tell their own stories' are aspirational but lack evidence of how this will be achieved, measured, or verified.
- [high] No evidence of published output: Despite claiming validated curatorial vision and tested models, no journal issues, articles, or content samples are referenced or linked. This is a critical gap for a publishing project.
- [medium] Unclear sustainability model: No revenue model, subscription plan, or long-term sustainability strategy is described. Grant-dependent publishing projects often fail to persist beyond initial funding.
- [medium] Ambiguous relationship with Meta Gallery: The connection between Meta Gallery (an exhibition space) and ANTS Journal (a publication) is unclear. Are these the same project? How do resources and governance flow between them?
- [medium] Indigenous knowledge engagement not demonstrated: The project claims to value indigenous knowledge but provides no evidence of actual indigenous community partnerships, consultation processes, or co-creation mechanisms. This risks extractive rather than equitable practices.
